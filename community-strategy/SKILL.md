# Community Strategy Skill

You are a community-led growth strategist. Your job is to help businesses grow through genuine
community engagement -- both by building owned communities and by earning trust in communities
where their ICP already congregates. You understand that community marketing is a long game:
shortcuts get you banned, but patience builds a pipeline that compounds.

## Inputs Required

### Required
1. **Business/product description** -- What you sell, to whom, current stage
2. **Goals** -- What community efforts need to achieve (awareness, trust, leads, retention, feedback)
3. **Target audience** -- ICP profile (from `icp-builder` if available, or docs/icp.md)

### Strongly Recommended
4. **Brand voice file** -- From `brand-voice` skill (docs/brand-voice.md) for consistent tone
5. **Existing community presence** -- Where you already participate, current follower counts, engagement levels
6. **Competitor community presence** -- Where competitors are active, what is working for them

### Optional
7. **Budget/capacity constraints** -- Team size, hours per week available for community work
8. **Product stage** -- Pre-launch, early traction, growth, scale (determines strategy emphasis)
9. **Content assets** -- Existing blog posts, guides, tools that can be shared as community value

## Output Contract
This skill produces exactly:
1. **Community audit** (10+ communities documented: platform, size, activity, ICP fit, opportunity score)
2. **Platform selection recommendation** (chosen platform with reasoning)
3. **Participation plan** (8-week trust-first timeline with week-by-week actions, 10:1 value-to-promotion ratio)
4. **Owned community design** (if applicable: naming, value proposition, launch sequence, rules, content calendar)
5. **Community content planning** (content types, cadence, discussion templates, AMA formats)
6. **Moderation guidelines** (daily routine, response SLAs, problem behavior handling, escalation)
7. **Community-to-pipeline conversion guide** (trust-first model, buying signal detection, exclusive tactics)
8. **Community-to-content pipeline** (mapping discussions to blog posts, case studies, social posts)
9. **Metrics framework** (core metrics with targets, corrective actions, pipeline attribution, monthly reporting)

This skill does NOT produce: Community platform setup, moderation execution, or content creation for distribution.

Before building or joining anything, find where your ICP already congregates.

### Prioritization

Rank communities by Opportunity Score and select:
- **Top 3-5 existing communities** to participate in (Phase 2)
- **1 platform** for building an owned community (Phase 3)

Do not spread across more than 5 external communities. Depth beats breadth.

## Phase 2: Existing Community Participation Playbook

This is the infiltration framework. The word is intentional -- you are entering someone else's
space and must earn the right to be heard. Rushing to promote gets you banned and damages your
brand. Follow the timeline strictly.

### The Trust-First Timeline

```
Weeks 1-2:    LURK           Read, observe, understand the culture
Weeks 3-4:    CONTRIBUTE     Answer questions, share genuinely helpful insights
Weeks 5-8:    ESTABLISH      Become a recognized, helpful member
Weeks 9-12:   SOFT MENTION   Reference your product ONLY when directly relevant
Week 13+:     EARNED TRUST   Community sees you as expert, not marketer
```

### Week-by-Week Playbook

**Weeks 1-2: Lurk and Learn**
- Read every post from the past 30 days
- Identify the top 10 most-asked questions
- Note the community tone, vocabulary, and inside jokes
- Identify power users and moderators by name
- Document the unwritten rules (what gets upvoted vs. ignored vs. removed)
- Do NOT post anything during this phase

**Weeks 3-4: Contribute Value**
- Answer 3-5 questions per week with genuinely helpful, detailed responses
- Share relevant resources (blog posts from other sites -- NOT yours yet)
- Ask thoughtful questions that show you understand the space
- Respond to other people's comments to build relationships
- Your bio/profile should mention your company, but your posts should not

**Weeks 5-8: Establish Expertise**
- Increase to 5-8 contributions per week
- Share original insights, frameworks, or data (not links to your content)
- Start longer discussions on topics where you have deep expertise
- Engage with moderators and power users directly
- Offer to help members with specific problems (via DM if appropriate)
- If the community allows, share your own blog/resource links when they genuinely answer a question -- but no more than once per week

**Weeks 9-12: Soft Product Mentions**
- When someone asks a question your product directly solves, mention it -- but lead with the solution, not the pitch
- Format: "Here is how to solve this: [detailed answer]. Full disclosure, I work on [product] which does this, but you can also do it manually by [alternative]."
- Maximum one product mention per week
- Continue contributing 5-8 non-promotional posts for every mention
- If anyone reacts negatively, back off immediately and return to pure contribution

**Week 13+: Earned Authority**
- You are now a known, trusted member
- Product mentions feel natural because the community knows you
- Maintain a 10:1 ratio of value posts to any product reference
- Consider offering community-exclusive discounts or early access
- Ask the community for product feedback (they will give it honestly)

### Participation Rules (Non-Negotiable)

1. **Never lead with a pitch.** Ever. Not even a soft one before Week 9.
2. **Never post the same message in multiple communities.** Each community gets original, context-specific content.
3. **Always disclose your affiliation** when discussing your product category.
4. **Never DM members unsolicited** with product pitches.
5. **Read and follow every community rule.** If a group says no self-promotion, that means zero -- not just a little.
6. **Be genuinely helpful even when it means recommending a competitor.** This builds more trust than any pitch.
7. **Respond to replies on your posts.** Dropping a link and disappearing is the hallmark of a spammer.

## Phase 3: Owned Community Design

Building your own community gives you control over the platform, the rules, and the relationship
with members. But it requires sustained effort -- a dead community is worse than no community.

### Build vs. Participate Decision Tree

Before committing to building an owned community, use this decision tree to determine the right approach for your stage:

- **IF <500 customers AND <1 year old** --> Do NOT build an owned community. Invest 100% of community effort into participating in existing communities where your ICP already gathers. You do not have the gravity to attract members yet.
- **IF 500-2,000 customers AND 20+ would join unprompted** --> Soft-launch an owned community alongside continued participation in existing communities. Start with a private beta group of your most engaged customers. Do not reduce participation effort in external communities.
- **IF 2,000+ customers AND a dedicated community person on staff** --> Full owned community build. You have enough density to sustain activity and a person to manage it daily. Continue light participation in external communities for awareness.
- **IF your ICP already congregates in 1-2 highly active communities** --> Participate first, regardless of your customer count. Build an owned community only if you outgrow what is available or need a space the existing communities cannot provide (e.g., customer-only support, product feedback loops).

When in doubt, participate. Building too early is one of the most common community strategy mistakes -- you end up with a ghost town that signals weakness rather than strength.

### Platform Selection Matrix

| Platform | Best For | Pros | Cons | Cost |
|----------|----------|------|------|------|
| **Facebook Group** | B2C, local businesses, non-technical audiences | Familiar UX, notifications built in, easy to find | Algorithm controls visibility, limited structure, you do not own the data | Free |
| **Discord** | Technical audiences, gaming, creator communities | Real-time chat, channels, roles, bots, integrations | Overwhelming for non-technical users, hard to search history | Free (Nitro optional) |
| **Circle** | Course creators, SaaS, professional communities | Threaded discussions, spaces, events, integrations | Paid ($89+/mo), less network effect than social platforms | $89-399/mo |
| **Slack** | B2B, professional/industry communities | Familiar to professionals, channels, integrations | 90-day message limit on free, expensive paid plans | Free-$12.50/user/mo |
| **Mighty Networks** | Coaches, creators, membership businesses | Courses + community in one, mobile app | Smaller user base, learning curve | $41-179/mo |
| **Custom forum** | Niche technical communities, brand control | Full ownership, SEO value, custom features | Development cost, adoption friction | Varies |
| **Skool** | Course creators, coaches | Simple, gamification, courses + community | Limited customization, growing platform | $99/mo |

**Decision framework:**
- If your ICP already uses Facebook daily -> Facebook Group
- If your ICP is technical/developer-oriented -> Discord
- If your ICP is B2B professional -> Slack or Circle
- If you sell courses or coaching -> Circle, Mighty Networks, or Skool
- If SEO value matters -> Custom forum (content is indexable)

### Community Naming

| Approach | Example | When to Use |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Brand-adjacent** | "[Brand] Community" | When brand is already known |
| **Topic-first** | "Service Business Growth Lab" | When brand is unknown; topic draws members |
| **Identity-first** | "The Operators Club" | When you want members to self-identify with a group |

**Rule:** If your brand has fewer than 1,000 customers, use topic-first or identity-first naming.
Nobody joins "[Unknown Brand] Community."

### Community Value Proposition

Members join for one or more of these reasons:

1. **Access** -- To experts, founders, or peers they cannot reach elsewhere
2. **Information** -- Insights, data, or knowledge not available publicly
3. **Connection** -- Meeting people like them who understand their challenges
4. **Accountability** -- Group structure that keeps them on track
5. **Status** -- Being part of an exclusive or recognized group

Define your community primary value proposition before launching. Write it as a single sentence:

```
"[Community name] is where [specific audience] [get specific value] so they can [achieve specific outcome]."
```

### Community Rules Template

Every community needs explicit rules. Post these in a pinned message or dedicated channel.

```
## Community Rules

1. BE HELPFUL FIRST -- Share knowledge generously. Help others before asking for help.
2. NO SPAM OR SELF-PROMOTION -- Do not post links to your products, services, or content
   without explicit permission or unless it directly answers a question.
3. STAY ON TOPIC -- Keep discussions relevant to [community topic].
4. BE RESPECTFUL -- Disagree with ideas, not people. No personal attacks.
5. NO UNSOLICITED DMs -- Do not DM members with pitches or offers.
6. SHARE WINS AND LOSSES -- Celebrate progress and be honest about setbacks.
7. SEARCH BEFORE ASKING -- Check if your question has been answered already.
8. GIVE CREDIT -- If sharing someone else's work, credit the source.

Violations: First offense = warning. Second = 7-day mute. Third = removal.
```

### Launch Sequence

1. **Pre-launch (2 weeks before):** Invite 20-30 founding members personally. These should be existing customers, colleagues, or industry contacts who will be active.
2. **Soft launch (Week 1):** Open to founding members only. Seed 5-10 discussion threads. Establish norms by example.
3. **Controlled growth (Weeks 2-4):** Invite existing email list in batches (50-100 at a time). Monitor engagement before scaling.
4. **Public launch (Week 5+):** Open registration. Add community link to website, email signatures, and content.

**Critical rule:** Never open to the public until you have at least 30 active members who post without prompting. A community with 500 silent members feels dead. A community with 30 active members feels alive.

## Phase 5: Moderation and Engagement Guidelines

### Daily Community Management Routine

| Time Block | Action | Duration |
|------------|--------|----------|
| **Morning** | Review new posts, approve pending members, respond to questions | 20-30 min |
| **Midday** | Post daily discussion prompt or content, engage with morning threads | 15-20 min |
| **Afternoon** | Respond to comments, DM new active members with welcome | 15-20 min |
| **Weekly** | Post member spotlight, plan next week content, review metrics | 45-60 min |

**Total time commitment:** 1-2 hours per day for a single community manager.

### Engagement Tactics

1. **Welcome every new member by name** within 24 hours
2. **Reply to every post** within the first 4 hours (even if just to acknowledge)
3. **Ask follow-up questions** on member posts to deepen discussion
4. **Tag relevant members** when a question matches their expertise
5. **Celebrate milestones** (member anniversaries, 100th post, achievement shares)
6. **Surface quiet members** by asking easy-to-answer questions they can contribute to

### Handling Problem Behavior

| Behavior | Response |
|----------|----------|
| Off-topic post | Move to appropriate channel/thread; gentle redirect |
| Self-promotion without permission | Remove post; DM member with rules reminder |
| Repeated self-promotion | 7-day mute; DM with final warning |
| Rude or disrespectful comment | Remove comment; DM with warning |
| Spam or scam | Immediate removal from community; no warning needed |
| Constructive criticism of your product | Thank them publicly; follow up with action taken |

## Phase 6: Community-to-Pipeline Conversion

This is where community work translates to business results -- but only if you protect trust.
The moment a community feels like a sales channel, members leave.

### The Trust-First Conversion Model

```
GIVE value consistently (Weeks 1-12)
    |
    v
EARN trust and authority
    |
    v
OBSERVE buying signals (questions about solutions, budget, timelines)
    |
    v
OFFER help (not pitches) -- "We built something for this, happy to show you"
    |
    v
CONVERT through demonstrated value, not persuasion
```

### Conversion Tactics (Ranked by Trust Impact)

| Tactic | Trust Impact | Conversion Potential | How |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|-----|
| **Exclusive early access** | Positive | High | Offer community members first access to new features or products |
| **Community-only pricing** | Positive | High | Special discount code only shared in community |
| **Office hours / live Q&A** | Neutral | Medium | Solve problems live; product naturally comes up |
| **Case study requests** | Positive | Medium | Ask successful members to share their story (with your product) |
| **Resource with soft CTA** | Neutral | Medium | Share a genuinely useful guide with a mention of your product at the end |
| **Direct pitch posts** | Negative | Low | Avoid. If you must, limit to 1x/month maximum in designated channels |

### Buying Signal Detection

Watch for these signals in community discussions:
- "Does anyone know a tool that does [thing your product does]?"
- "I am comparing [your category] options right now"
- "We have outgrown our current [solution]"
- "How do you handle [problem your product solves] at scale?"
- "What is your budget for [your product category]?"

When you spot a signal: respond with a helpful, detailed answer first. Mention your product second. Always offer a no-pressure way to learn more (link to docs, free trial, or a call).

## Phase 7: Community as Content Source

Active communities generate raw material for content across all channels.

### Community-to-Content Pipeline

| Community Signal | Content Output | Channel |
|-----------------|---------------|---------|
| Frequently asked question | Blog post or FAQ entry | Blog, knowledge base |
| Heated discussion thread | Newsletter deep-dive or opinion piece | Newsletter, LinkedIn |
| Member success story | Case study | Blog, sales collateral |
| Poll results | Data-driven social post or infographic | Social media, blog |
| Common complaint or pain point | Product feedback ticket or feature request | Product team |
| Expert AMA highlights | Blog post summary or video clips | Blog, YouTube, social |
| Challenge results | Roundup post with participant quotes | Blog, newsletter |
| Resource recommendations | Curated list post | Blog, newsletter |

### Process for Turning Discussions into Content

1. **Flag high-value threads** weekly during your community review
2. **Ask permission** before quoting members by name (or anonymize)
3. **Add your expert perspective** -- do not just repackage; add analysis
4. **Credit the community** -- "This post was inspired by a discussion in [community name]"
5. **Share the finished content back** to the community -- they contributed, they should see the result

### Feedback Loop to Product

Community discussions are unfiltered product research. Establish a process:

```
Community discussion surfaces pain point
    |
    v
Document in product feedback log (tag: community-sourced)
    |
    v
Share with product team weekly
    |
    v
If feature ships, announce in community FIRST (members feel ownership)
```

### Downstream Handoff Table

Community strategy outputs feed directly into other skills. Use this table to route insights:

| Output | Consuming Skill | How It Is Used |

## Phase 8: Metrics and Measurement

### Core Community Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target (Healthy Community) |
|--------|-----------|---------------------------|
| **Member growth rate** | New members per week/month | 5-15% month-over-month |
| **Active member %** | Members who posted or commented in last 30 days | 20-30% of total members |
| **Engagement rate** | (Reactions + comments + posts) / total members | 10-25% weekly |
| **Response time** | Average time to first reply on a member post | Under 4 hours |
| **Content generation** | Posts and comments created by members (not admins) | 60%+ member-generated |
| **Retention rate** | Members still active after 90 days | 40-60% |
| **NPS / satisfaction** | Quarterly survey: "How likely to recommend this community?" | 50+ NPS |

### Corrective Actions When Targets Are Missed

Do not just track metrics -- act on them. When a core metric falls below target, apply these corrective actions:

| Metric Below Target | Corrective Actions |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Active member % < 20%** | Post more discussion prompts (increase to daily). Tag silent members in easy-to-answer threads. Host a live AMA or Q&A to re-engage lurkers. Run a 5-day challenge with low barrier to entry. DM the 10 most recently inactive members and ask what would make the community more useful. |
| **Response time > 4 hours** | Assign a dedicated morning check-in (first 30 min of the day) and evening check-in (last 15 min). Set up mobile notifications for new posts. Recruit 2-3 active members as volunteer moderators who can answer in off-hours. Create a pinned "quick questions" thread that gets monitored more frequently. |
| **Retention rate < 40%** | Survey departing members (short 3-question exit survey or DM). Audit content quality -- is the community delivering on its value proposition? Review the onboarding experience -- are new members getting welcomed and oriented? Check if a small group is dominating conversation and making others feel excluded. Introduce a "30-day new member" program with guided engagement. |
| **Engagement rate < 10%** | Vary content formats -- try polls, images, short videos instead of only text posts. Post at different times of day to find when members are most active. Ask more open-ended questions that invite personal experience rather than yes/no answers. Feature and celebrate member contributions more visibly. |
| **Content generation < 60% member-generated** | Reduce admin posting frequency and replace with prompts that invite member responses. Create recurring threads owned by members (e.g., "Friday Wins" posted by a rotating member). Ask members to share their expertise on specific topics. Publicly thank and highlight every member-generated post. |

### Pipeline Attribution Metrics

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

These are the most common ways community marketing efforts fail. Each includes a concrete BAD vs. GOOD example so you can recognize and prevent the behavior.

### 1. The Drive-By Poster

Dropping a link or announcement and disappearing without engaging in the conversation.

- **BAD:** "Hey everyone! I'm the founder of FieldKit and we solve exactly this problem. Check us out at gofieldkit.com!"
- **GOOD:** "We ran into this exact issue at our company. Here's what worked: [detailed steps explaining the solution, the reasoning, and the tradeoffs]. Happy to elaborate on any of these."

The BAD version contributes nothing. The GOOD version is useful even if the reader never visits your site.

### 2. The Fake Helper

Answering questions with surface-level advice that always pivots to a product pitch.

- **BAD:** "Great question! Scheduling is really hard for service businesses. That's actually why we built FieldKit -- it handles all of this automatically. Want a demo?"
- **GOOD:** "Great question. Here are three ways to handle same-day reschedules: [Option A with pros/cons], [Option B with pros/cons], [Option C with pros/cons]. We have tried all three and Option B worked best for crews under 10 people because [specific reason]."

The test: would your answer still be valuable if you deleted the last sentence? If not, rewrite it.

### 3. The Copy-Paster

Posting identical content across multiple communities without adapting to context, tone, or rules.

- **BAD:** Posting the same "5 Tips for Field Service Efficiency" message word-for-word in r/HVAC, r/Plumbing, a Facebook Group, and a Slack workspace.
- **GOOD:** Writing a unique response for each community that reflects its specific vocabulary, concerns, and culture. r/HVAC gets HVAC-specific examples. The plumbing group gets plumbing-specific examples. Each post references recent discussions in that community.

Moderators across communities talk to each other. Getting flagged as a copy-paster in one group can get you preemptively banned in others.

### 4. The Premature Promoter

Mentioning your product before earning trust -- any time before Week 9 of the Trust-First Timeline.

- **BAD:** (Week 2, second-ever post in the group) "Has anyone tried FieldKit for dispatching? We just launched and I'd love feedback."
- **GOOD:** (Week 10, after 40+ helpful posts) "Here's how to solve this: [detailed manual process]. Full disclosure, I work on FieldKit which automates this part, but the manual approach works fine for smaller teams."

The community will discover your product through your profile and your expertise. Let them come to you.

### 5. The Dead Community Launcher

Opening an owned community with a big invite blast but zero seeded content, resulting in members arriving to an empty room.

- **BAD:** Emailing 2,000 customers "Join our new community!" on day one. Members arrive, see zero posts, and never return.
- **GOOD:** Inviting 25 founding members two weeks before launch. Seeding 10+ discussion threads. Having conversations already in progress before the first batch of public invites goes out.

You only get one first impression. A community that looks dead on arrival is nearly impossible to revive.

### 6. The Metrics Obsessor

Tracking vanity engagement numbers (likes, member count) instead of measuring relationship quality and business impact.

- **BAD:** Celebrating "We hit 1,000 members this month!" while only 15 people have posted anything and zero community-sourced leads have entered the pipeline.
- **GOOD:** Reporting "Active member percentage is 28%, up from 22%. Three community discussions surfaced pain points that informed our Q2 positioning. Two members converted to customers after 8+ weeks of engagement."

A 200-member community with 30% active participation is worth more than a 5,000-member community with 2% activity.
