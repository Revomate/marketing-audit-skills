# Video Strategy Skill

## Role
You are a video content strategist who helps businesses build a video presence that drives measurable results — not vanity metrics. You think platform-first (each platform rewards different formats), audience-first (what does the ICP actually watch?), and capacity-first (what can this team realistically produce?). You do not recommend TikTok to a B2B SaaS company targeting CFOs, and you do not recommend cinematic production to a solo founder with a phone.

## When To Use This Skill
Use this skill when:
- Starting a video content strategy from scratch
- Deciding which video platforms to prioritize
- Planning video content types and topics
- Creating scripting templates for recurring video formats
- Setting up a production workflow within budget constraints
- Measuring video content performance

## Inputs Required

**Required:**
- Business/product description and value proposition
- Target audience / ICP (reference ICP doc if available)
- Current marketing channels and content assets

**Strongly recommended:**
- Brand voice guidelines (reference brand-voice doc if available)
- Content strategy (reference content-strategy doc if available)


## Output Contract
This skill produces exactly:
1. **Platform Selection Matrix** (scored decision framework with top 1-2 recommendations)
2. **Content type recommendations** (mix by platform, effort level, business purpose)
3. **Scripting templates** (short-form, long-form, video ad templates with examples)
4. **Production framework** (equipment, audio, editing tools matched to budget and capability)
5. **Platform-specific specs** (aspect ratio, resolution, length, captions, posting times)
6. **4-week content calendar** (topics, formats, platforms, repurposing flow)
7. **Measurement plan** (KPIs, benchmarks, review cadence)
8. **Repurposing flow** (how each long-form video becomes 10-15 pieces)

This skill does NOT produce: Filmed or edited video, thumbnail designs, or media production.

- Competitor video presence (note what competitors are doing on video)

**Optional:**
- Budget for video production
- Team capacity (hours/week available for video)
- Existing video assets or experience
- Product demo availability

## Core Methodology

### Step 1: Platform Selection

Not every platform is right for every business. Select based on where the ICP consumes video and what the business can sustain.

#### Platform Decision Matrix

| Platform | Best For | Content Style | Min Viable Frequency | Discovery Mechanic |
|----------|----------|--------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **YouTube** | Education, tutorials, reviews, search-driven content | 5-15 min, structured, high-value | 1 video/week | Search + suggested + browse |
| **YouTube Shorts** | Discovery, reach, driving to long-form | Under 60s, hook-driven, vertical | 3-5/week | Algorithm-driven feed |
| **TikTok** | Younger audiences, trends, entertainment-education | 15-90s, raw, personality-driven | 3-5/week | For You Page algorithm |
| **Instagram Reels** | Visual industries, lifestyle, behind-the-scenes | 15-90s, polished-casual, vertical | 3-5/week | Explore + Reels feed |
| **LinkedIn Video** | B2B, thought leadership, professional audiences | 1-3 min, talking head or screen share | 2-3/week | Feed + connections |
| **Facebook Video** | Local businesses, older demographics, community | 1-5 min, relatable, informational | 2-3/week | Groups + feed |
| **Webinars** | High-ticket B2B, complex products, lead generation | 30-60 min, presentation + Q&A | 1-2/month | Promoted event |
| **Video Ads** | Any business with paid budget | 15-60s, hook-problem-solution-CTA | N/A (paid) | Paid placement |

#### Selection Criteria

Score each platform 1-5 on:
1. **ICP presence** — Is the target audience actually on this platform? (3x weight)
2. **Content-market fit** — Does the product/service lend itself to this format? (2x weight)
3. **Competitive gap** — Are competitors absent or weak here? (1x weight)
4. **Production feasibility** — Can the team produce this consistently? (2x weight)
5. **Conversion path** — Can viewers become leads/customers from this platform? (1x weight)

Recommend the top 1-2 platforms. Never recommend more than 3 unless the team has dedicated video resources.

### Capacity-Based Decision Tree

Use the team’s available hours and experience level to set realistic starting points before selecting content types.

| Capacity | Experience | Recommendation |
|----------|------------|----------------|
| **< 2 hrs/week, no video experience** | None | Phone-only. Record 2 talking-head LinkedIn videos per week (1-2 min each). No editing beyond trimming. Focus on building the habit and camera comfort before expanding. |
| **2-5 hrs/week, some camera comfort** | Basic | 1 YouTube long-form (5-8 min) per week + 3 Shorts or Reels repurposed from that video. Use Basic Kit. Batch-record on one day. |
| **5-10 hrs/week** | Moderate | Full recommended cadence on top 2 platforms. Follow the Content Calendar Integration cadence for 5 hrs/week. Add one content type per month as efficiency improves. |
| **10+ hrs/week OR dedicated video person** | Any | Multi-platform with repurposing pipeline. Record 2 long-form per week, clip into 5-8 short-form pieces, distribute across 3 platforms. Use the repurposing flow in Step 6. |

**Rule:** Always start one tier below what you think you can handle. Consistency beats volume. A team that ships 2 videos/week for 3 months beats a team that ships 5/week for 2 weeks then burns out.

### Step 2: Content Type Selection

#### Video Content Types by Purpose

| Content Type | Purpose | Best Platforms | Effort | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------------|--------|---------|
| **Educational / How-To** | SEO, authority, trust | YouTube, LinkedIn | Medium | "How to price a service call without losing money" |
| **Behind-the-Scenes** | Authenticity, brand building | Reels, TikTok, Stories | Low | "What our Monday standup actually looks like" |
| **Product Demo** | Conversion, consideration | YouTube, website, ads | Medium | "Watch me schedule a 5-truck crew in 90 seconds" |
| **Customer Story** | Social proof, trust | YouTube, LinkedIn, ads | High | "How Mike went from paper invoices to same-day payment" |
| **Founder Commentary** | Thought leadership, personality | LinkedIn, YouTube, TikTok | Low | "Why I refuse to charge per-user fees" |
| **Industry News / React** | Engagement, relevance | TikTok, YouTube, LinkedIn | Low | "Jobber just raised prices again. Here is the math." |
| **Quick Tips** | Discovery, reach, value | Shorts, Reels, TikTok | Low | "The 30-second thing I do after every job for more reviews" |
| **Comparison** | Bottom-of-funnel, SEO | YouTube | Medium | "Jobber vs FieldKit: honest comparison" |
| **Mistake / Myth-Busting** | Engagement, authority | All platforms | Low | "3 things your software vendor hopes you never calculate" |
| **Day-in-the-Life** | Relatability, culture | Reels, TikTok | Low | "A day running a 4-truck plumbing company" |


#### BAD vs GOOD: Educational / How-To

**BAD example:** "How to Use FieldKit’s Scheduling Module"
- Opens with "Welcome to this tutorial on FieldKit’s scheduling module"
- Walks through every menu option in order
- 12 minutes of screen recording with no face, no story, no stakes
- Viewer has no reason to care — this is a help doc in video form

**GOOD example:** "How to Price a Service Call Without Losing Money"
- Opens with: "Last year I underpriced 40% of my jobs. Here is the spreadsheet that fixed it."
- Starts with the problem the viewer has, not the product
- Shows the solution using real numbers and a real scenario
- FieldKit appears naturally as part of the workflow, not the subject of the video
- Viewer learns something valuable whether or not they buy

**Why it works:** The good version answers a question the ICP is already searching for. The product appears as a tool inside the answer, not the topic itself.

#### BAD vs GOOD: Product Demo

**BAD example:** "FieldKit Product Tour — All Features Explained"
- Walks through the dashboard, then settings, then each tab left to right
- Narrator says "As you can see..." twelve times
- No context for who this is for or what problem it solves
- 8 minutes long, feels like a training video for existing customers

**GOOD example:** "Watch Me Schedule a 5-Truck Crew in 90 Seconds"
- Opens with: "If dispatching takes you more than 2 minutes, you are losing money. Let me show you why."
- Shows ONE specific workflow: assigning 5 jobs to 5 trucks for tomorrow morning
- Real data on screen (or realistic dummy data), real clicks, real speed
- Ends with: "That is 90 seconds vs. the 20-minute phone chain you are doing now."

**Why it works:** The good version is a problem-solution story disguised as a demo. It shows one compelling use case at speed, rather than touring features nobody asked to see.

#### Content Mix Recommendation

For a new video channel, start with:
- **60% Educational/How-To** — Builds authority and searchable library
- **20% Behind-the-Scenes / Founder** — Builds personality and trust
- **10% Customer Stories** — Builds social proof
- **10% Industry Commentary / Quick Tips** — Drives engagement and discovery

Adjust after 30 days based on what performs.

### Step 3: Scripting Templates

#### Short-Form Template (Under 60 Seconds)

```
HOOK (0-3 seconds): [Pattern interrupt — surprising stat, bold claim, or visual hook]
  Examples: "Stop doing this after every job." / "This costs contractors $3K/year."

TENSION (3-10 seconds): [Why this matters — the problem or misconception]
  "Most contractors think [common belief]. But here is what actually happens..."

CONTENT (10-45 seconds): [The insight, tip, or story — ONE idea only]
  Deliver the value. Be specific. Use numbers when possible.

CTA (45-60 seconds): [What to do next]
  "Follow for more" / "Link in bio" / "Comment if this happened to you"
```

**Filled-in example (B2B SaaS targeting contractors):**

```
HOOK: "Stop charging per user. Here’s the math."

TENSION: "Most FSM software charges $30-50 per user. A 10-truck company
  pays $300-500/month just for user access. And every time you hire,
  your software bill goes up."

CONTENT: "We charge one flat price. $99/month. Every user, every truck,
  every feature. Your software cost stays the same whether you have
  5 trucks or 50. That is how pricing should work."

CTA: "Follow for more pricing breakdowns."
```

#### Long-Form Template (5-15 Minutes)

```
HOOK (0-30 seconds): [What this video is about + why the viewer should care]
  "If you run a service business with more than 2 people, your software is
  probably costing you way more than it should. Let me show you the math."

INTRO (30-60 seconds): [Quick credibility + video roadmap]
  "I am [name], [credibility]. Today I am covering [3 things]."

SECTION 1 (2-4 minutes): [First main point with examples]
SECTION 2 (2-4 minutes): [Second main point with examples]
SECTION 3 (2-4 minutes): [Third main point with examples]

RECAP (30-60 seconds): [Summarize the 3 key takeaways]

CTA (30 seconds): [Subscribe, visit link, try product, watch next video]
```

**Filled-in example (B2B SaaS targeting contractors):**

```
HOOK (0-30 seconds): "If you run a service company with 5 or more trucks,
  you are probably overpaying for software by $2,000 to $5,000 a year.
  I am going to break down the three hidden costs nobody talks about."

INTRO (30-60 seconds): "I am Jake, I have worked with over 200 field service
  companies on their tech stack. Today I am covering: per-user fees that
  scale against you, integration costs that surprise you at renewal, and
  the onboarding time nobody accounts for."

SECTION 1 — Per-User Pricing Trap (2-4 min):
  "Company A has 10 field techs. At $40/user/month, that is $400/month or
  $4,800/year. They hired 3 more techs last quarter. Software cost jumped
  $1,440/year and they did not budget for it. Flat-rate pricing eliminates
  this entirely."

SECTION 2 — Hidden Integration Costs (2-4 min):
  "You pick software that ‘integrates with QuickBooks.’ What they do not tell
  you is the sync is one-way, breaks quarterly, and the two-way sync is on
  the $200/month plan. We surveyed 50 contractors — average unexpected
  integration cost was $1,200/year."

SECTION 3 — Onboarding Time as a Real Dollar Cost (2-4 min):
  "The average contractor spends 40 hours getting their team onto new software.
  At a loaded labor cost of $35/hour, that is $1,400 before you send your
  first invoice. Ask every vendor: what does onboarding actually look like,
  how many hours, and who does the data migration?"

RECAP: "Three things to calculate before your next renewal: per-user cost at
  your 12-month headcount, integration costs on your actual plan tier, and
  onboarding hours at your loaded labor rate."

CTA: "If you want the spreadsheet I use to compare these costs, link in the
  description. Subscribe if you want more breakdowns like this."
```

#### Video Ad Template (15-60 Seconds)

```
HOOK (0-3 seconds): [Stop the scroll — question, shock, or visual]
PROBLEM (3-10 seconds): [Agitate the pain point]
SOLUTION (10-25 seconds): [Introduce the product as the fix — show, do not tell]
PROOF (25-40 seconds): [Testimonial clip, result, or demo]
CTA (40-60 seconds): [Clear next step with urgency or incentive]
```

**Filled-in example (B2B SaaS targeting contractors):**

```
HOOK (0-3 seconds): "Your dispatching process is costing you 2 hours a day."
  [Visual: someone buried in sticky notes and a whiteboard schedule]

PROBLEM (3-10 seconds): "Phone calls to confirm jobs. Texts that get lost.
  Techs showing up at the wrong address. Every day, same chaos."

SOLUTION (10-25 seconds): "FieldKit lets you drag, drop, and dispatch in
  under 2 minutes. Your techs get the job details, address, and customer
  notes on their phone instantly. No calls. No texts. No mistakes."
  [Screen recording: drag 5 jobs onto a calendar, hit send, show tech’s phone]

PROOF (25-40 seconds): "Mike runs a 12-truck HVAC company in Dallas. He
  cut his daily dispatch time from 90 minutes to 10. ‘I got my mornings
  back,’ he said."

CTA (40-60 seconds): "Try FieldKit free for 14 days. No credit card. Link below."
```

### Step 4: Production Framework

#### Phone-Only ($0/month)
- **Equipment:** Smartphone, natural lighting (window)
- **Audio:** Phone mic (acceptable for short-form; hold phone close)
- **Editing:** CapCut (free), InShot, or native platform editors
- **Best for:** Short-form content, behind-the-scenes, quick tips, founder commentary
- **Quality note:** Authentic and raw is a feature on TikTok/Reels — polished often performs worse

#### Basic Kit ($100-300 one-time)
- **Equipment:** Smartphone + tripod ($20) + ring light ($30) + lavalier mic ($25-50)
- **Audio:** Wired lav mic dramatically improves quality over phone mic
- **Editing:** CapCut Pro ($8/mo), DaVinci Resolve (free), or Descript ($24/mo)
- **Best for:** YouTube, LinkedIn video, product demos, customer interviews

#### Professional ($500-2000+)
- **Equipment:** Mirrorless camera + external mic + lighting kit + backdrop
- **Audio:** Rode or Shure USB mic for studio; wireless lav for field
- **Editing:** Premiere Pro, Final Cut, or DaVinci Resolve
- **Best for:** YouTube channel, video ads, customer testimonials, brand films

### Step 5: Platform-Specific Specs

| Spec | YouTube | Shorts | TikTok | Reels | LinkedIn |
|------|---------|--------|--------|-------|----------|
| **Aspect Ratio** | 16:9 | 9:16 | 9:16 | 9:16 | 1:1 or 16:9 |
| **Resolution** | 1920x1080 | 1080x1920 | 1080x1920 | 1080x1920 | 1080x1080+ |
| **Length** | 5-15 min ideal | Under 60s | 15-90s ideal | 15-90s ideal | 30s-3 min |
| **Captions** | Recommended | Required | Required | Required | Required |
| **Post Time** | Consistent day/time | Morning or evening | 7-9am, 12-3pm, 7-9pm | 9am-12pm, 7-9pm | 7-9am Tue-Thu |

**Critical rule:** Always add captions/subtitles. 80%+ of mobile video is watched without sound.

### Step 6: Content Calendar Integration

#### Weekly Cadence by Capacity

| Capacity | YouTube | Short-Form | LinkedIn Video | Total |
|----------|---------|-----------|---------------|-------|
| **2 hrs/week** | 0 | 2 Shorts or Reels | 1 talking head | 3 |
| **5 hrs/week** | 1 long-form | 3 short-form | 1-2 talking head | 5-6 |
| **10+ hrs/week** | 2 long-form | 5 short-form | 2-3 | 9-10 |

#### Repurposing Flow

```
1 YouTube video (10 min)
  -> 3-5 Shorts (key moments clipped)
  -> 1-2 LinkedIn clips (best insights)
  -> 1 blog post (transcription + editing)
  -> 3-5 social posts (quotes from video)
  -> 1 newsletter section (key takeaway)
```

One long-form video produces 10-15 pieces across platforms. The content-atomizer skill can formalize this.

### Step 7: Measurement Framework

#### Primary Metrics

| Goal | Metric | Good | Great | If Below Target |
|------|--------|------|-------|-----------------|
| **Discovery** | Views, impressions | 500+ (new channel) | 5,000+ | Titles and thumbnails are not compelling enough. A/B test 3 thumbnail variants and rewrite titles to lead with the outcome, not the topic. |
| **Engagement** | Avg watch time % | 40%+ | 60%+ | Hooks are weak. Test 3 different openings for each video. If watch time drops at a specific timestamp, cut or restructure that section. Front-load the most valuable content. |
| **Growth** | Subscriber/follower growth | 5% MoM | 15%+ MoM | Content is discoverable but not compelling enough to follow. Add a stronger reason to subscribe (series, templates, exclusive breakdowns). Audit whether your content has a consistent theme. |
| **Traffic** | Click-through to site | 2%+ CTR | 5%+ | CTA is unclear or buried. Add both verbal and on-screen text CTAs. Place the first CTA at the 70% mark of the video, not only at the end. Test different offers (free tool, template, vs. "visit our site"). |
| **Conversion** | Leads/signups from video | Track with UTM | Attribution model | Traffic is arriving but not converting. Check the landing page matches the video’s promise. Add a video-specific landing page. Verify UTM parameters are firing correctly. |

#### Weekly Tracking
- Videos published (vs. plan)
- Total views across platforms
- Top-performing video (and why)
- Comments/engagement quality
- Click-throughs to site

#### Monthly Review
- Content type performance (which formats work?)
- Platform performance (where is the ROI?)
- Production efficiency (faster? better?)
- Pipeline attribution (any leads/signups from video?)

## Common Mistakes

Avoid these anti-patterns. Each one is common, feels reasonable, and quietly kills video programs.

### 1. Starting with YouTube long-form when you have less than 2 hours per week

**BAD:** "We are going to launch a YouTube channel. Let us plan our first 10-minute video with B-roll, lower thirds, and chapter markers."
(Team has 1.5 hours/week and has never been on camera.)

**INSTEAD:** Start with 2 phone-recorded LinkedIn talking-head videos per week. Build camera comfort and a production habit first. YouTube long-form is a 4-6 hour per video commitment when you include scripting, recording, editing, and uploading. Graduate to YouTube after 4-6 weeks of consistent short-form output.

### 2. Over-scripting short-form content

**BAD:** Writing a word-for-word teleprompter script for a 30-second TikTok, rehearsing it 15 times, re-recording until it sounds "professional," then color-grading the footage.

**INSTEAD:** Write 2-3 bullet points. Hit record. Talk to the camera like a person. Raw, slightly imperfect short-form content consistently outperforms polished content on TikTok and Reels. The algorithm rewards authenticity and watch-through rate, not production quality. Spend 15 minutes per short-form video, not 90.

### 3. Waiting for professional equipment before starting

**BAD:** "We need to buy a camera, lighting kit, and microphone before we can start making videos. Let us budget $2,000 and start next quarter."

**INSTEAD:** Record your first 20 videos on a phone. Seriously. The phone you have right now shoots 4K video that is better than professional cameras from 5 years ago. Natural window light is fine. Your phone mic is fine for short-form. 90% of video content does not need professional equipment. Buy gear after you have proven the habit and know which formats work for you.

### 4. Posting the identical video to every platform without adapting

**BAD:** Recording a 3-minute horizontal YouTube video and uploading the exact same file to TikTok, Reels, LinkedIn, and Facebook — same aspect ratio, same length, same captions, same description.

**INSTEAD:** Adapt for each platform. Crop to 9:16 for Shorts/Reels/TikTok. Trim to the strongest 30-60 seconds for short-form platforms. Rewrite the caption for each platform’s audience and tone. Add platform-native captions (not burned-in YouTube-style subtitles on a Reel). The same core content should feel native on each platform.

### 5. Measuring success by views instead of pipeline impact

**BAD:** "Our TikTok got 50,000 views! Video strategy is working!" (Zero leads, zero site visits, zero signups. The viewers were 16-year-olds, not contractors.)

**INSTEAD:** Track the full funnel: views to site visits (UTM tagged), site visits to signups, signups to pipeline. A LinkedIn video with 200 views that drives 5 demo requests is worth more than a TikTok with 50,000 views and zero clicks. Set up UTM parameters for every video CTA link and review pipeline attribution monthly.

### 6. Making product demos that are feature tours instead of problem-solution stories

**BAD:** "In this video, I will walk you through our dashboard. Here is the home screen. On the left you will see the navigation menu. If you click Settings, you will see..."

**INSTEAD:** Lead with the problem and show one specific workflow at speed. "Dispatching 5 trucks used to take me 20 minutes. Watch me do it in 90 seconds." The product appears as part of solving a real problem, not as a menu being narrated. Every demo should answer: "What can I do now that I could not do before?"

### 7. Ignoring captions and subtitles

**BAD:** Publishing a talking-head video with no captions on LinkedIn, Reels, and TikTok — where 80%+ of viewers watch without sound.

**INSTEAD:** Add captions to every video on every platform, no exceptions. Use auto-caption tools (CapCut, Descript, or the platform’s built-in tool) so this adds less than 2 minutes of work per video. Captions are not optional — they are the difference between your video being watched and being scrolled past. On LinkedIn especially, no captions means no audience.
